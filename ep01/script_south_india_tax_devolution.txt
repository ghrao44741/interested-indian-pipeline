Karnataka contributes nearly nine percent of India's GDP with about five percent of its population. For every rupee it sends to the union government in taxes, it gets back roughly fifteen paise. Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Telangana, and Andhra Pradesh face versions of the same arithmetic. The states doing the most economically are the ones losing the most fiscally. That is not an accident of geography. It is the output of a formula.

To understand why, you have to start with Article 280 of the Constitution. It created the Finance Commission, a body reconstituted every five years to decide how the union government splits its tax revenue with the states. This is not a courtesy. It is a structural necessity. Under India's constitutional design, the union collects the bulk of major taxes, income tax, corporation tax, GST. States carry most of the spending responsibility, health, education, policing, rural infrastructure. Someone has to bridge that gap. The Finance Commission is that bridge.

Every commission answers two questions. First, how much of the shared tax pool goes to states as a whole, the vertical share. Second, how that pool is split between individual states, the horizontal share. The first question is about the union versus the states. The second is about states versus each other. And it's the second question that produces the paradox playing out in Bengaluru, Chennai, and Thiruvananthapuram right now.

The Fourteenth Finance Commission, submitted in 2015, set the vertical share at forty-two percent, the highest it had ever been. States cheered. But the horizontal formula behind that pool was already shifting in a direction the southern states would come to regret. The Fifteenth Finance Commission, covering 2021 to 2026, brought that shift into sharp focus. It lowered the vertical share slightly to forty-one percent. More consequentially, it changed how population was counted.

Every Finance Commission needs a population figure to help decide who needs more money. For decades, that figure came from the 1971 census, frozen deliberately. Why freeze it. Because using a current census would reward states with high birth rates and effectively fine states that had successfully brought population growth under control. Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Karnataka had done exactly that. Starting in the 1970s, these states ran aggressive family planning programs, decades before the rest of the country followed. By the 2000s their fertility rates had dropped near or below replacement level. Uttar Pradesh and Bihar had not made the same progress.

The Fifteenth Finance Commission was told, under its terms of reference, to use the 2011 census instead of the 1971 figures. That single instruction, buried in a government notification, is the mechanism behind the entire dispute. Once 2011 population data entered the formula, states with larger, faster-growing populations gained share. States that had spent forty years suppressing their birth rates lost it.

The numbers make the mechanism concrete. Under the Fourteenth Finance Commission, Karnataka's share of the divisible tax pool was 4.713 percent. Under the Fifteenth, it fell to 3.647 percent. That is a drop of roughly twenty-three percent in the state's allocation, translating into a cumulative shortfall state officials have put above eighty thousand crore rupees across the award period. Kerala lost roughly a quarter of a percentage point of the pool as well, a smaller-sounding number that still runs into thousands of crores annually. Uttar Pradesh, by contrast, retained the largest single share of any state, at nearly eighteen percent, and Bihar's allocation continued to rank second nationally in absolute terms.

Why did southern states lose out specifically. The formula the Fifteenth Commission used weighted several factors together. Population, at fifteen percent. Area, at fifteen percent. Forest and ecology cover, at ten percent. Income distance, at forty-five percent, meant to give more to states with lower per capita income relative to the richest state. Demographic performance, at twelve and a half percent, meant to reward states with lower fertility rates, intended as a partial correction for the population shift. Tax and fiscal effort, at two and a half percent.

On paper, the demographic performance criterion looks like it should have protected the southern states. In practice, its weight was too small to offset the switch to 2011 population data, and the income distance criterion, the largest single factor, worked against states like Karnataka and Tamil Nadu precisely because their higher per capita income made them look less in need of support under the formula's own logic. A state can generate more GST, house more corporate headquarters, and post stronger growth, and still come out behind in this calculation, because the formula treats income level as evidence of lower need rather than evidence of contribution.

This is where the political reaction becomes explainable rather than just loud. In 2024, the chief ministers of Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Telangana appeared together in Delhi to protest the devolution formula, an unusual show of coordinated action across states governed by different parties. Karnataka's chief minister, Siddaramaiah, has repeatedly cited the fifteen-paise figure in public forums, framing it not as an abstract fiscal grievance but as a direct penalty for responsible governance. Kerala's former finance minister, Thomas Isaac, made a similar argument years earlier, when the shift to 2011 population data was first proposed, warning that it would punish exactly the states that had done what national population policy asked of them.

The union government's counterargument follows constitutional logic rather than political convenience. The Finance Commission's mandate is not to reward efficient states. It is to equalize fiscal capacity across a union with enormous income disparities between states. Uttar Pradesh's per capita income is a fraction of Karnataka's. Bihar's is lower still. If devolution followed contribution rather than need, poorer states would fall further behind, and the union would stop functioning as a union in any meaningful sense. Both positions are internally consistent. They are simply optimizing for different things, one for reward, one for redistribution, and the formula can only pick one weighting at a time.

There's a second layer to this that rarely makes it into the political rhetoric. Devolution isn't the only channel of transfer. The union also distributes centrally sponsored schemes, discretionary grants, and disaster and revenue deficit grants, and those channels operate under separate, less transparent rules. A state can lose share in the formal Finance Commission formula and still receive compensating amounts through other routes, which is part of why the union describes the net picture as more balanced than the raw devolution percentages suggest, while state governments describe those other channels as unpredictable and politically discretionary rather than a stable entitlement they can plan a budget around.

The introduction of GST in 2017 added a further complication. Before GST, states had independent power to set and collect certain taxes. GST folded most of that into a single national pool, administered jointly but collected centrally, then redistributed through the GST Council rather than the Finance Commission. States gave up fiscal autonomy in exchange for a promised compensation mechanism, guaranteed for five years, which expired in 2022. Southern states, again disproportionately consumption and services heavy in their tax base, argue that the post-compensation GST regime compounds the same devolution problem, reducing their independent revenue tools just as the Finance Commission formula was already working against them.

The Sixteenth Finance Commission, constituted in November 2023 under the chairmanship of Arvind Panagariya, is now working through these same tensions for the award period beginning April 2026. Early indications suggest the vertical share will again be held near forty-one percent, and several states, including Karnataka, have submitted formal proposals asking for the income distance weighting to be reduced and replaced with a criterion based on each state's direct contribution to national GDP. Preliminary estimates already show Karnataka's inter-se share moving upward again, from 3.647 percent to just over four percent, though still below its Fourteenth Commission level, suggesting the formula is drifting back toward the states it took share away from without fully reversing course.

What this dispute really exposes isn't a flaw in the Finance Commission. It's the underlying tension the Constitution built in on purpose. A single national tax pool, distributed by formula, will always create winners and losers, because no formula can simultaneously reward economic performance and correct for economic inequality. Every five years, a new commission sits down, adjusts the weights, and shifts the outcome in one direction or the other. The states on the losing side of that adjustment change, but the argument itself, contribution versus need, has been running since Article 280 was first drafted.

So the paradox from the beginning of this video isn't really a paradox at all. It's the visible output of a formula working exactly as constructed, redistributing revenue away from the states that grew fastest and toward the states that grew slowest, because growing slower was, in the framework's own terms, defined as needing more. Karnataka's fifteen paise on the rupee isn't a bureaucratic error. It's the constitutional bargain of a diverse union, written into a spreadsheet, and recalculated every five years.
