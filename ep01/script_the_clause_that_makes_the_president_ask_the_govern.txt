So here's something I learned last week that I still can't quite believe. If the Prime Minister wants to dismiss a state government in India, they can't just — do it. Instead, the President has to send a formal letter to the Governor, who then has to politely inform the Chief Minister that their government no longer enjoys confidence, and could they perhaps consider stepping down. It's like if your boss wanted to fire you but had to send your mom a recommendation that maybe you should quit. This is a real constitutional procedure. It has happened thirty-seven times since independence. And I need to explain why it exists, because the story behind it is somehow more absurd than the process itself.

Let me start with what this actually is. Article 356 of the Indian Constitution allows the President to impose what's called "President's Rule" on any state. When this happens, the elected state government is suspended, the Chief Minister is out, and the state is run directly by the central government through the Governor. On paper, this is supposed to be an emergency measure for when a state government literally cannot function — like if there's a complete breakdown of law and order or the legislature is so fractured nobody can form a majority.

In practice, it's been used ninety-one times between 1950 and 2023. Ninety-one. For context, there have been seventeen Lok Sabhas in that same period. We've had more President's Rule impositions than general elections. And here's the thing that made me go back and re-read the Constitution three times — the actual wording of Article 356 never defines what "breakdown of constitutional machinery" means. It just says the President can impose it if they're "satisfied" that the state government can't function. Satisfied based on what? The Constitution doesn't say.

Now before you think this is just states whining about the central government being mean to them, let me give you a few examples of how this has actually been used. In 1959, the central Congress government dismissed the Communist government in Kerala — a government that had won a clear majority in a free election. The official reason was law and order breakdown. The real reason was that Kerala's education reforms were making the Congress party nervous. In 1977, after the Emergency ended and Janata Party won the center, they immediately dismissed nine Congress state governments, including Tamil Nadu and Punjab, even though those governments had perfectly functioning majorities. The stated reason this time was that those assemblies "no longer reflected the will of the people" because the national mood had changed. Which is not actually a constitutional justification, but here we are.

The most brazen one might be Arunachal Pradesh in 2016. The Congress had a majority. There was an internal party dispute about who should be Chief Minister. The Governor, using powers I didn't even know Governors had, advanced the assembly session date on his own, then said the government had lost confidence because of this internal party mess. The center imposed President's Rule. The Supreme Court later said this was completely illegal and reversed it, but not before the central government had successfully helped engineer a floor-crossing operation. The constitutional machinery had broken down, apparently, because politicians were arguing about who should lead them, which — I mean, that's just called politics.

Here's where the choreography gets properly weird. The President cannot impose President's Rule on their own initiative. They need a report from the Governor saying things are broken. The Governor is appointed by the President, on the advice of the Prime Minister. So the chain of command is: Prime Minister tells President who to appoint as Governor, Governor sends report to President saying state government should be dismissed, President acts on that report and dismisses the government. It's constitutional buck-passing as a formal procedure.

And then there's the parliamentary approval bit. After the President imposes President's Rule, both houses of Parliament have to approve it within two months. If they don't, President's Rule automatically lapses. But here's the thing — if the party in power at the center has a majority in Parliament, which they obviously do because they're in power, then this approval is essentially automatic. It's not a real check. It's a constitutional formality designed to look like accountability.

I know this sounds like boring constitutional procedure stuff, so let me tell you why this actually matters. In the 1970s and 80s, President's Rule was imposed thirty-six times. That's an average of once every ten months for two decades. It became the standard central government tactic for dealing with opposition-ruled states. Win an election in a state? Cool, the center will just dismiss your government six months later. The Supreme Court finally got so tired of this in 1994 that they laid down guidelines in the S.R. Bommai case saying you can't just impose President's Rule because you feel like it — there has to be actual objective evidence of constitutional breakdown, and courts can review it.

After Bommai, the frequency dropped. Between 1994 and 2023, President's Rule has been imposed twenty-three times — still a lot, but less cartoonishly frequent. And several of those were genuinely for hung assemblies where no party could form a government, which is arguably what the provision was meant for in the first place.

But the Bommai judgment created its own absurdity. Now when the center wants to impose President's Rule, the Governor's report has to be very carefully worded to make it sound like objective constitutional breakdown rather than political convenience. So you get these incredibly elaborate reports listing things like "rising incidents of communal tension" or "deteriorating law and order situation" with attached statistics that may or may not actually show deterioration depending on which baseline you use. It's like writing a Yelp review designed to survive legal scrutiny. The Governor becomes a constitutional lawyer trying to draft around precedent.

Here's my favorite part of this whole mess. The Governor, who is the central figure in this entire process, has no fixed term. They serve "at the pleasure of the President" — which in practice means at the pleasure of the Prime Minister. So if a Governor refuses to send a report recommending President's Rule, the center can just replace them with someone who will. This happened in Tamil Nadu in 1991. The Governor refused to recommend President's Rule. The center removed him and appointed a new Governor, who promptly sent the report. The Supreme Court later said the dismissal was invalid, but again, not before the political damage was done.

Now let me explain why the Constitution set it up this way, because the framers weren't idiots. The original logic goes back to the Government of India Act 1935, which the British used to govern colonial India. That act had a similar provision called Section 93, which allowed the Governor-General to take over provinces if the local government failed. The constitutional assembly in 1949 debated whether to include something similar. B.R. Ambedkar, who chaired the drafting committee, explicitly warned that Article 356 should be a "dead letter" — meaning it should exist for genuine emergencies but never actually be used. He said, and I'm quoting here, "I hope the President will take proper precautions before actually dismissing the ministry."

The assumption was that the President would act as a neutral constitutional arbiter, not a party political figure. The assumption was that Governors would be elder statesmen above partisan politics. The assumption was that Parliament would provide meaningful oversight. All three of those assumptions turned out to be wrong within a decade of independence.

And here's the thing that genuinely bothers me about this — there's a weird constitutional imbalance baked in here. If a Chief Minister has a majority in the state assembly, they cannot be removed by a vote of no confidence unless MLAs actually cross the floor and vote them out. That's a high bar. It requires public defection, horse-trading, all the ugly but visible mechanics of democratic politics. But Article 356 lets the center bypass all of that. The Governor sends a report, the President signs an order, and the democratically elected government is gone. No floor test required. No public vote. Just executive action wrapped in constitutional language.

The counter-argument, which I should mention because some of you are already typing it, is that without Article 356, there's no mechanism to deal with genuinely broken state governments. And that's fair. In 1992, after Babri Masjid was demolished, the UP government had clearly failed to maintain law and order. President's Rule there was arguably justified. In 2005, Bihar had a hung assembly and literally no combination of parties could form a majority — President's Rule was the only option until fresh elections. The provision isn't inherently illegitimate. It's the frequency and context of its use that creates the problem.

But here's what I keep coming back to. We have a federal system where states are supposed to have independent executive authority in their domains. The whole point of federalism is that states aren't just administrative units of the center — they're autonomous political entities with their own elected governments. And yet we have a constitutional clause that lets the center suspend that autonomy with basically no advance judicial review. The Supreme Court can reverse it afterwards, like they did in Arunachal, but by then the political crisis has already done its work.

The current UPA-era Sarkaria Commission recommended in 1988 that Article 356 should only be used as a last resort, and only when objective material shows constitutional breakdown. The National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution in 2002 went further and suggested Article 356 should be amended to require a two-thirds majority in Parliament, not just a simple majority, to approve President's Rule. Neither recommendation has been implemented.

So where does this leave us? We have a constitutional provision designed for emergency use that became a routine political tool, then got partially restrained by courts, but still exists as this Sword of Damocles hanging over every opposition-ruled state government. Governors have become the most powerful unelected officials in Indian federalism because they control access to this nuclear option. And the whole process is wrapped in this elaborate constitutional courtesy where the President must ask the Governor to inform the Chief Minister, as if adding procedural steps makes the power less blunt.

I think the reason this bothers me is that it reveals a deep ambivalence in the Indian constitutional design about whether we actually trust federal democracy. If we trust it, why do we need a clause that lets the center overrule state voters? If we don't trust it, why pretend we have federalism at all? The constitutional assembly in 1949 couldn't resolve that tension, so they built Article 356 as a compromise — a federal override switch that's supposed to exist but never be touched. Except we've touched it ninety-one times.

And so we're left with this: a constitutional procedure where the President asks the Governor to ask the Chief Minister to maybe resign, because formally acknowledging that the center can just fire state governments would make the whole federal structure look like theater. Which, depending on how you count Article 356 impositions, it sort of already is.
